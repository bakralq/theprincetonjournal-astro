The Texas Department of Transportation is proposing to improve **US 380 from FM 1827 to CR 560 in Collin County**, primarily by building a **new-location freeway that loops north of Princeton** and reconnects to existing US 380 near Lake Lavon.

The goal is to transition this stretch of US 380 toward a **controlled-access freeway facility** that can better handle current and future traffic, rather than continuing as a signalized surface highway.

## What Is Being Built

• A **new-location, controlled-access freeway** will be built north of the City of Princeton, roughly from west of **CR 337** to east of **CR 458**, where it will tie back into the existing corridor.

• The project length is approximately **11.8 miles**, or about **12 miles**, from **FM 1827 to CR 560**.

• The concept is an **eight-lane controlled-access freeway** with continuous **two- to three-lane, one-way frontage roads**. The number of mainlanes may vary from **8 to 10** depending on the location.

• **10-foot shared-use paths** are planned on both sides of the corridor for walking and biking.

• The project also reconstructs and widens existing US 380 from **FM 1827 to west of CR 337**, and from **east of CR 458 across Lavon Lake to CR 560**.

This is not just a basic widening of the current road. In several areas, it is a **completely new alignment** that would function as a real freeway with limited driveways and intersections, higher travel speeds, and stronger separation between local traffic and through traffic.

## Why TxDOT Is Doing This

• The current US 380 does **not meet modern freeway standards**.

• Traffic has grown rapidly alongside Collin County’s population boom and is expected to continue increasing.

• TxDOT’s stated goals are to **reduce congestion, improve safety, and prepare for long-term east-west traffic demand**.

## Where The Project Stands

• TxDOT completed the **Environmental Assessment** and posted a **Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI)** on **January 6, 2025**, allowing the project to move beyond environmental clearance.

• The project remains in the **development and design phase**. Public meetings and hearings have already been held.

• TxDOT’s public hearing materials state that the project is **currently unfunded for construction** and cannot proceed to construction letting until funding is identified, although right-of-way work may still move forward.

• Next steps include **final design work, right-of-way acquisition, utility relocation, and phased construction** if and when funding becomes available.

## Property and Right of Way

• Because much of the freeway will follow a **new alignment**, TxDOT will need significant additional right-of-way.

• Some properties will be affected. TxDOT materials state that additional right-of-way is required and that **displacements are anticipated**, depending on final design.

• Approximately **396 acres of new right-of-way** are expected to be needed.

TxDOT’s right-of-way process generally includes **appraisals, purchase offers, and relocation assistance where required by law**.

## What It Will Look Like On The Ground

When built, this section of US 380 is expected to include:

• **8 to 10 mainlanes**, depending on the segment, for higher-speed travel

• Continuous **two- to three-lane, one-way frontage roads** for local access

• **10-foot shared-use paths** on both sides

In practical terms, this would move US 380 in this area away from a congested highway with traffic lights and toward a **full urban-style freeway corridor**, similar to how other major Texas routes are built.

## Why This Matters for Princeton

This project could reshape how Princeton functions for decades.

A new freeway alignment north of the city would affect:

• how local residents move east and west

• how traffic is divided between local access roads and regional through traffic

• where future commercial and residential growth concentrates

• how land values and development patterns evolve along both the current and future corridors

It is one of the most important long-term infrastructure projects affecting Princeton’s future.
