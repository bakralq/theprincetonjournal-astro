## Overview

Based on verified public data from **January through October 2025**, including Princeton Police Department call totals, FBI NIBRS violent-crime data for Collin County, and the **FY 2025–2026 police budget**, a clearer picture of Princeton’s public-safety trends begins to emerge.

This article summarizes the key patterns.

For readers who want a short version, see **“Quick Read”** at the end.

---

## Overall Activity

Princeton PD handled approximately **3,112 calls through October**, averaging about **315 incidents per month** across twelve major reporting categories.

For a city with fewer than **50,000 residents**, that represents a significant operational workload.

### Traffic Crashes

Traffic collisions remain one of the largest operational pressures for officers.

Monthly crash totals in 2025:

• January: 54  
• February: 45  
• March: 62  
• April: 82  
• May: 77  
• June: 57  
• July: 70  
• August: 99  
• September: 66  
• October: 73  

Princeton consistently records **45 to nearly 100 crashes per month**, with **reckless-driving calls ranging from 24 to 52 monthly**.

---

## Violence in Princeton

Violent incidents in Princeton are **overwhelmingly domestic or relational**, rather than random acts between strangers.

Combined **domestic disturbance and assault calls**:

• January: 36  
• February: 27  
• March: 32  
• April: 33  
• May: 52  
• June: 42  
• July: 49  
• August: 34  
• September: 43  
• October: 46  

These patterns align with **FBI NIBRS data for Collin County**, where most aggravated assaults and homicides occur **inside residences** and involve **partners, family members, or acquaintances**.

---

## Property Crime Patterns

Property crime activity shows **seasonal spikes rather than constant trends**.

### Burglaries
Monthly totals ranged from **3 to 26**, with a large spike in **April (26 incidents)**.

### Vehicle Theft
Monthly totals ranged from **2 to 12**, peaking in:

• May: 10  
• October: 12

### Vehicle Burglaries
Activity remained relatively low for most of the year before rising to **9 cases in October**.

These fluctuations match broader **county seasonal patterns**, suggesting short periods of concentrated activity rather than continuous growth.

---

## Quality-of-Life Calls

Many police calls involve **non-violent community disturbances**, but these calls consume significant officer time.

Examples include:

**Noise complaints:**  
18 to 112 per month, peaking in July.

**Disturbance calls:**  
21 to 49 per month.

**Alarm calls:**  
41 to 66 per month.

Although these incidents rarely appear in headlines, they represent a substantial share of daily police workload and tend to **increase alongside population growth**.

---

## County Violent-Crime Context (FBI NIBRS)

While the FBI data reflects **Collin County as a whole**, it provides context for the type of violent crime typically seen in this region.

**Aggravated assault:**  
Approximately 38 victims, mostly adults ages 20–59. Most incidents occurred inside homes and involved known offenders.

**Homicide:**  
Four cases in the dataset, all occurring in residences. Weapons included a handgun, blunt object, and firearm.

**Rape:**  
Victims were mostly ages 10–19, with incidents typically occurring in residences and involving acquaintances.

**Robbery:**  
Five offenders and three victims, mostly strangers. Firearms were present in several incidents.

The FBI cautions that crime data should **not be used to rank cities**, as the numbers reflect reporting patterns rather than comparative scores.

## Police Budget and Staffing

According to the **FY 2025–2026 proposed city budget**:

• Police and School Resource Officer funding increases from **$9.10M to $9.49M**  
• Full-time personnel increase from **49 to 51 positions**

Department priorities include:

• Maintaining response times  
• Keeping violent crime below the five-year average  
• Expanding proactive enforcement  
• Managing increased traffic and growth pressures

---

## What the Data Shows

After reviewing the available data:

• Violence in Princeton is **primarily domestic**, not random  
• **Traffic crashes** remain a major operational challenge  
• **Burglaries and vehicle crime** show seasonal spikes  
• **Quality-of-life calls** create a large portion of daily workload  
• **Police funding and staffing are increasing** as the city grows

Princeton is **not a high-violence city**, but it is a **rapidly expanding community with heavy call volume**.

Future progress will depend on aligning resources with the patterns visible in the data.

## Quick Read

Princeton logged **3,112 police calls from January through October 2025**, with **45–99 crashes per month** and frequent reckless-driving incidents creating major workload pressure. Violence is mostly domestic rather than random, with domestic disturbance and assault calls ranging from **27 to 52 monthly**, reflecting county trends where serious incidents often occur inside homes between known individuals. Property crime shows seasonal spikes, including **26 burglaries in April**, vehicle theft peaks in **May (10)** and **October (12)**, and **9 vehicle burglaries in October**. Quality-of-life calls such as noise complaints, disturbances, and alarms remain consistently high each month. Meanwhile, the **FY 2025–2026 city budget increases police funding from $9.10M to $9.49M and staffing from 49 to 51 officers** to manage traffic, domestic incidents, and continued population growth.

**Bottom line:** Princeton is not a violent city, but a **busy and fast-growing one with predictable pressure points.**
