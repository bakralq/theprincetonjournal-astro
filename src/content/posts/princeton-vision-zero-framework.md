On Monday, January 12, Princeton City Council will hear a presentation on **Vision Zero** as part of the city’s first **Mobility and Safety Action Plan**.

The item is scheduled as a **work session discussion** on the pros and cons of issuing a Vision Zero proclamation, presented by **Wes Pierce**.

## What Vision Zero Is

Vision Zero is a traffic-safety approach built around the goal of **eliminating traffic fatalities and serious injuries**.

It treats deadly crashes as **preventable rather than inevitable** and places a higher priority on safety than on speed or convenience.

## What It Would Mean for Princeton

If Princeton adopts the framework, it would shape how the city:

• identifies high-risk locations  
• prioritizes safety projects  
• tracks results over time  
• uses a more data-driven approach to traffic safety

## What It Does Not Do by Itself

A Vision Zero proclamation is a **policy commitment**.

By itself, it would **not automatically change speed limits, redesign roads, or install new safety infrastructure**.

Any specific projects or physical changes would still need to come later through:

• the Mobility and Safety Action Plan  
• annual budgets  
• future City Council actions

## Why Residents Should Care

Traffic-safety policy affects where the city directs attention and funding over time.

A Vision Zero commitment could influence how Princeton prioritizes:

• road safety projects  
• pedestrian crossings  
• trail connections  
• traffic calming measures  
• future grant-supported transportation improvements

Even though a proclamation would not change roads on its own, it could affect which areas are studied first, which projects move forward faster, and which improvements become more competitive for outside funding.

## Why Staff Is Recommending It

According to Public Works, Vision Zero could improve safety for:

• pedestrians  
• cyclists  
• drivers

Staff also states that the framework could strengthen the city’s eligibility and competitiveness for certain state and federal transportation safety grants, depending on program rules, particularly where those grants are aimed at improving both safety and mobility.

## Grant Funding Mentioned in the Presentation

The presentation references several funding programs commonly associated with Vision Zero-style planning and implementation:

• **Safe Streets and Roads for All**  
A U.S. Department of Transportation program that funds safety action plans and implementation projects designed to prevent roadway deaths and serious injuries

• **Highway Safety Improvement Program**  
A core federal-aid program focused on reducing traffic fatalities and serious injuries through a data-driven approach

• **Road to Zero Community Traffic Safety Grants**  
A grant program funded by NHTSA to support projects advancing the goal of zero traffic deaths
