Since January 2023, Princeton ISD has altered how it handles legal representation and begun exploring external partnership models, leaving a visible paper trail in BoardBook.

## January 26, 2023

Trustees voted **5–2** (per Princeton Herald coverage) to hire **Timothy Davis of Cantey Hanger** as a separate board attorney while retaining **Abernathy, Roeder, Boyd & Hullett (ARBH)** as district counsel.

In the same meeting, the agenda also included policy items on campus and program charters. This reflects the board’s move to retain separate counsel; it does not by itself split legal duties beyond the board having its own attorney.

## May 15, 2023

The regular meeting agenda listed **“Charter Partnership.”**

This is the first clear public signal, visible in posted agendas, of interest in outside partnership models.

## February to April 2024

The topic appears repeatedly and becomes more explicit:

• **Feb. 26 (Regular Meeting):** “TFER Partnership.”  
• **Apr. 4 (Workshop):** “Discuss TFER Partnership.”  
• **Apr. 15 (Regular Meeting):** “TFER Partnership Follow-Up Meeting.”

Separately, on **Apr. 25**, trustees posted a **tour of Responsive Education Solutions (ResponsiveEd)**, further evidence that outside partnership models were being actively explored.

*(TFER stands for Texas Foundation for Education Rights, a nonprofit advocating partnership and school choice options.)*

## May 19, 2025

The agenda included items to:

• Terminate the **Perdue Brandon delinquent-tax contract**, and  
• Enter a **contingent-fee contract with ARBH for delinquent receivables**.

This would shift delinquent tax collection work to the district’s primary law firm.

## Public access today

As of **Oct. 17, 2025**, Princeton ISD’s BoardBook includes agendas and many minutes from **2023 through 2025**, including regular meetings and workshops.

We found **no official record indicating that minutes were permanently removed and later restored**.

## Legislative context

**SB 1882**, often referred to as the Texas “Texas Partnerships” law, provides the state framework commonly cited for district–partner models.

However, the Princeton ISD agendas referenced above **do not explicitly cite SB 1882**.

Readers should distinguish between advocacy framing, such as references to TFER, and the specific actions formally posted or voted on by the board.

## Bottom line

Public records show that Princeton ISD:

• Added **separate board counsel** while continuing to retain ARBH as the district’s primary legal firm.  
• Explored **partnership or charter-style operational models** across multiple meetings, including references to TFER and a ResponsiveEd tour.  
• On **May 19, 2025**, posted agenda items to terminate the Perdue Brandon delinquent-tax contract and approve a new contingent-fee agreement with ARBH for delinquent receivables.

All of the documents referenced are available in **BoardBook for public review**.

## Why This Matters

Taken together, these actions suggest a subtle shift in how Princeton ISD distributes and exercises institutional authority.

Hiring separate board counsel gives trustees an independent legal voice. That structure is common in larger districts but less typical in smaller ones, and it signals a board asserting greater autonomy from district administration.

Exploring charter-style or partnership frameworks could allow outside entities to operate or influence district programs under state law flexibility. Depending on how partnerships are structured, this could either introduce new innovation or shift decision-making away from traditional locally elected oversight.

Meanwhile, consolidating tax-collection work with the district’s primary law firm centralizes both financial enforcement and legal strategy under the same institutional relationship.

None of these actions are inherently positive or negative. They are structural decisions rather than procedural ones.

They determine who advises the board, who might operate future programs or campuses, and who enforces revenue collection.

For a fast-growing community like Princeton, those choices shape the long-term balance between **local governance, external partnerships, and public accountability**.

In other words, the story here is not controversy but **direction**.

Princeton ISD appears to be gradually reorganizing itself for a new phase of governance, partnership exploration, and growth.

Residents who follow these records closely will understand not only what has changed but also where the district may be headed next.
