The Princeton City Council will meet Tuesday, October 14, 2025 at 6:30 PM at City Hall, with a work session beginning at 5:30 PM.

The Princeton Journal reviewed every page of the agenda and supporting documents, more than 25,000 words in total, to break down what’s happening, what it means for Princeton, and why it matters.

## Street Report

The city evaluated every mile of roadway in Princeton, 177 miles total. About 85 percent of streets were rated in good or satisfactory condition, while only a small number were classified as poor or failed.

This report creates a data-based system for prioritizing repairs and repaving so that future road work targets the areas that need it most.

## Multi-Generational Recreation Center

Council will choose the funding level for the recreation center approved by voters under the 2023 Parks Bond.

The working budget was originally $75 million, but the options now being considered are $50 million or $60 million. That decision will determine the size and amenities that move forward into final design.

## Winter Quarter Averaging (WQA)

Council will discuss and potentially adopt a change to how sewer bills are calculated.

Under Winter Quarter Averaging, sewer charges would be based on average winter water usage between December and February. The idea is to prevent summer lawn watering from increasing sewer bills.

Until winter averages are established, sewerable gallons would be capped at 10,000 per month.

## Project Harvard (Chick-fil-A Site)

The Chick-fil-A development project continues moving forward.

The city plans to contribute up to $150,000 toward construction of a required turn lane and median along U.S. 380. The funding would come from roadway impact fees, which are collected from development and legally restricted to infrastructure improvements tied to growth.

## Princeton Pediatrics

The Princeton Pediatrics clinic at 136 South 2nd Street has been built, but its final plat is up for a vote.

A final plat is the legal document that establishes lot lines, easements, and other development requirements.

City staff recommends denial due to unresolved fire code, drainage, and maintenance bond requirements.

The developer’s engineer says drainage issues have been addressed and that the Texas Department of Transportation has no remaining hydrology or hydraulic concerns. Staff maintains that other city standards remain unmet.

The Planning and Zoning Commission recommended denial on September 15. Council can deny the plat, approve it with conditions, or table the item.

## Whitewing Trails PID

Council will consider setting a public hearing for November 10 regarding new assessments in the Whitewing Trails Public Improvement District.

The proposal covers Areas 3A and 3B, approximately 595 homes. Each home would carry an assessment ranging from about $23,000 to $28,000. These assessments are paid over time and fund infrastructure such as roads, drainage, and utilities.

Developers also pay a one-time fee of $2,615 per home to the city when the bonds are issued.

## City Finances and Banking

Council will consider designating SouthState Bank as the city’s primary depository through 2030.

According to staff scoring, SouthState offered stronger interest earnings. The council will also consider extending the current People’s Bank contract through September 30, 2026 to ensure a smooth transition.

## City Hall Cleaning Contract

Council will consider awarding a new contract to Fresh Local Cleaning for up to $136,500 per year.

The proposal includes a daily on-site porter and two exterior window cleanings each year.

## Kiddie Academy

Council will review a preliminary plat for a childcare facility east of Monte Carlo Boulevard on a 2.099 acre site.

City staff and engineers recommend approval. The Planning and Zoning Commission unanimously recommended approval on September 15.

## CCAD Board Nominations

Princeton will nominate up to two candidates for the Collin Central Appraisal District Board, which helps oversee property valuation across Collin County.

Tonight’s action would only make nominations. Official ballots are distributed later and votes are cast by December.

## Executive Session

Council will enter executive session to discuss Princeton Professional Park, likely involving real estate or legal matters. Any official vote or direction must occur afterward in open session.

## In Summary

Tomorrow’s meeting covers major decisions that shape Princeton’s future, from how sewer bills are calculated to how much the city invests in parks, roads, and everyday services.

Each item ultimately connects back to how Princeton grows, spends public money, and manages accountability.
