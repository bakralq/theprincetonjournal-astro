Princeton’s pause on new residential development, first adopted September 23, 2024, will expire November 30, 2025 after two City Council extensions.

City officials say the pause allowed staff to reassess infrastructure capacity and development standards during a period of record growth. At this time, the moratorium is not expected to be extended further.

## Why the Moratorium Existed

Between July 2023 and July 2024, Princeton was the fastest growing city in the United States, expanding approximately 30.6 percent according to the U.S. Census Bureau.

That pace of growth placed pressure on roads, drainage, utilities, and public safety infrastructure.

The moratorium temporarily halted new residential approvals so city staff and engineers could evaluate system capacity and update development standards.

Some of that scrutiny has already affected projects under review. On September 15, the Planning and Zoning Commission voted 4–0 to recommend denial of the Princeton Pediatric Addition final plat (FP20244618), citing drainage and fire code concerns.

City Council is scheduled to consider that item at the October 14 meeting.

## What Changed Under State Law (HB 2559)

House Bill 2559, which took effect September 1, 2025, significantly limits how Texas cities can impose or extend development moratoria.

Under the new law, cities must now complete several steps before adopting a moratorium:

• Two public hearings held at least 30 days apart  
• Two ordinance readings held at least 28 days apart  
• Final adoption requiring a three fourths vote of the City Council  

Each moratorium can last no more than 180 days, and cities cannot adopt another moratorium covering the same issue or geographic area for two years.

Princeton’s final extension, Ordinance No. 2025-06-23 adopted June 23, 2025, established November 30, 2025 as the expiration date.

The city’s official moratorium webpage confirms that timeline.

## What Happens After November 30

Unless City Council adopts a new and more narrowly tailored moratorium within the limits allowed by HB 2559, the city will resume accepting and approving new residential plats under existing development regulations.

Projects that were paused during the moratorium are expected to return to Planning and Zoning Commission and City Council agendas during the winter months.

However, drainage capacity, fire safety requirements, and infrastructure impacts are expected to receive closer scrutiny going forward.

## When and Where to Watch

• **City Council Meetings:** 6:30 PM on the second and fourth Mondays of each month (November 10 and November 24)  
• **Planning & Zoning Commission:** 6:30 PM on the first and third Mondays (November 3 and November 17)  

**October 14 Special Council Meeting:** Includes the pavement condition report and Whitewing Trails PID assessments. As of publication, the Princeton Pediatrics item was not listed on the agenda.

The full moratorium timeline and related documents are available through the city’s Orderly Growth Ordinance and Moratorium information page.

## The Takeaway

November 30 represents a turning point for Princeton.

With House Bill 2559 limiting the ability to impose broad citywide moratoria in the future, managing growth will likely rely on stricter enforcement of development codes, project phasing, and conditional approvals rather than blanket pauses.

The coming months will show whether Princeton’s development reset leads to stronger infrastructure oversight or whether the city’s rapid growth resumes under tighter regulatory conditions.
