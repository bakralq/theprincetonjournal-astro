## Overview

On **December 8**, the Princeton City Council is scheduled to vote on **Ordinance 2025-12-08-13**.

If adopted, the ordinance would change how the city calculates **residential sewer bills** by introducing **winter quarter averaging**.

For readers who want the short version, see **“Quick Read”** at the end.

---

## What This Means in Plain Language

Under the proposed ordinance, **winter quarter averaging** would use a household’s **average water use in December, January, and February** to determine the wastewater portion of the bill for the rest of the year.

Instead of following peak summer usage, sewer billing would be tied more closely to **winter indoor water use**.

Winter water use tends to reflect water that actually goes down household drains, while much of the additional summer water is used for irrigation and landscaping.

---

## Step-by-Step for a Typical Home

• Residents would still pay a **monthly base sewer fee**.

• Until a winter average is established, the sewer volume would equal **that month’s metered water use**, but **never exceed 10,000 gallons**.

• Once the winter quarter average is calculated, the city would use that average as the sewer volume for future billing, subject to the **same 10,000-gallon cap**.

This approach attempts to align sewer charges with **indoor wastewater use rather than outdoor watering patterns**.

---

## Who This Could Help

If adopted, winter quarter averaging would mainly benefit:

• Households that **water lawns or landscaping heavily in summer**  
• Larger households whose **summer water use is high but winter indoor use is moderate**

For these residents, sewer bills could become **more stable and better aligned with actual indoor usage**.

Residents whose **winter water use is already high** may see little change in their monthly sewer costs.

---

## What City Staff Warned About

City staff have told Council that winter quarter averaging is a **significant financial decision** for the wastewater utility fund.

Sewer rates help pay for:

• Treatment through the **North Texas Municipal Water District**  
• **Daily operations and system maintenance**  
• **Repairs and infrastructure expansion** as Princeton continues growing

Changing the billing formula could affect **future rate studies, project planning timelines, and long-term fund stability**.

Staff also noted that once the billing structure is implemented in the city’s software systems, **reversing it later can be complicated**.

---

## What Residents Should Do If It Passes

If winter quarter averaging is adopted, Princeton sewer customers should treat **December, January, and February as key billing months**.

Residents may want to:

• Monitor water use carefully during those months  
• Fix leaks quickly  
• Avoid unnecessary water waste  
• Keep winter bills for reference

If a **leak or one-time emergency** causes unusually high water use during one of those months, residents should **contact Utility Billing quickly and document the issue**, since a single spike could affect the average used for the entire year.

---

## Quick Read

Princeton City Council is scheduled to vote on adopting **winter quarter averaging** for sewer billing. If approved, residential sewer charges would be based on **average water use during December, January, and February** instead of peak summer usage, with sewer volume still capped at **10,000 gallons per month**. Until a winter average is established, sewer charges would follow **actual metered water use up to the same cap**. The change could reduce bills for households with heavy summer irrigation but also affects the wastewater utility fund and long-term infrastructure planning. If adopted, residents should pay close attention to **water use and leaks during the winter months**, since those three months will determine sewer billing for the rest of the year.
