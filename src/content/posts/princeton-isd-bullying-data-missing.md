Bullying affects student safety, mental health, attendance, and learning.

Princeton ISD has policies and tools in place, but the public cannot evaluate how well they are working because key information is not publicly reported.

This post lays out what is known, what is provided, and what is missing.

## 1. Princeton ISD does not publish public bullying statistics

As of January 2026, Princeton ISD does not publish centralized public data showing:

• how many bullying reports are filed  
• how many are investigated  
• how many are substantiated  
• what actions are taken  
• whether incidents are rising or falling

The district’s Required Postings page includes the anti-bullying policy, but it does not include a public bullying report, dashboard, or annual summary.

Without this data, families cannot see campus trends, compare years, or evaluate whether district responses are effective.

## 2. Princeton ISD provides reporting tools

The district uses **tip411** for anonymous reporting of bullying and other safety concerns.

The district also has a formal grievance process that allows bullying complaints to be escalated from the campus level to the superintendent and then to the school board.

These systems exist.

What the district does not publish is how often they are used or what outcomes they produce.

## 3. Counseling services are listed, but results are not reported

Princeton ISD lists counseling services intended to support students and respond to behavioral concerns.

The district does not publish data showing:

• how many bullied students receive counseling  
• what interventions are used  
• whether repeat incidents decrease  
• whether students report feeling safer afterward

The services are listed. The outcomes are not.

## 4. Community members say bullying is happening

Parents and residents in local Princeton community groups have publicly stated that bullying is occurring and that families are frustrated with how it is being handled.

Some have said their own children have experienced bullying in Princeton ISD schools.

These are public statements reflecting ongoing community concern.

## 5. At least one student left school after harassment

A Texas Observer report describes a former Princeton High School student who experienced repeated harassment related to gender expression and neurodiversity, including incidents at school and online.

According to that report, the student eventually stopped attending and later pursued a GED.

This is a documented example of why outcomes matter.

## 6. Bullying is widely underreported by students

Students often do not report bullying because of:

• fear of retaliation  
• social consequences  
• a belief that nothing will change

Because of this, districts cannot rely on reporting tools alone.

Prevention also depends on adults recognizing and stopping bullying when they see it.

## 7. Staff intervention is critical but not publicly tracked

Princeton ISD does not publish information showing:

• how staff are trained to identify bullying  
• what staff are required to do when they witness it  
• whether staff intervention is monitored or audited

If harassment is dismissed as joking or ignored in real time, bullying can continue even when reporting systems exist.

## 8. No public bullying transparency report

Princeton ISD does not release regular public reports showing:

• bullying incidents by campus  
• investigations and outcomes  
• disciplinary actions  
• repeated locations or patterns  
• changes over time

Without this, the community cannot meaningfully evaluate whether conditions are improving.

## 9. Records exist, but are not proactively shared

Princeton ISD allows public records requests for bullying-related information under Texas law.

But families must request data individually.

The district does not proactively publish high-level summaries, dashboards, or recurring public accountability reports.

## What this means

Princeton ISD has policies, reporting tools, and counseling services.

What it does not provide is public, measurable accountability for bullying.

Without transparency on incidents, staff intervention, and outcomes, families cannot know whether students are being protected.
