A local contractor recently sent The Princeton Journal a simple question:

Who does the City of Princeton do business with, and how are those vendors selected?

No accusations were made. No names were named. No proof was claimed.

The concern raised was whether the city may be relying on a single HVAC contractor repeatedly, and whether the public can easily understand why.

We reviewed publicly available purchasing policies, Texas municipal procurement law, and the city’s posted financial and payment data.

Here is what we found.

## What We Reviewed

To evaluate the concern, this review examined publicly accessible materials, including:

• the City of Princeton’s published purchasing information and policy materials  
• Texas municipal procurement law, including Chapter 252 of the Texas Local Government Code  
• publicly available city financial and payment reporting, including check register archives  
• other city pages and documents relevant to procurement and financial reporting  

We also attempted to evaluate the exclusivity question using the payment and contracting information that is currently available online.

## What We Found

### 1. The city has formal procurement rules

The City of Princeton states that it follows the State of Texas procurement framework for municipalities and also publishes purchasing information and policies.

Texas law sets baseline competitive procurement requirements for municipalities. This review notes that Texas updated the general Chapter 252 competitive procurement threshold to $100,000 effective September 1, 2025, and cities may adopt stricter internal requirements than the state minimum.

The framework exists.

The public-interest question is whether that framework is documented and presented in a way that residents can easily verify.

### 2. Princeton is in a region served by multiple HVAC providers

Princeton is located in a regional market where multiple HVAC and mechanical providers operate.

If the city were primarily using one HVAC contractor, that pattern would not generally be explained by a lack of available vendors. This is an observation about market availability, not a claim about any specific vendor relationship.

### 3. Vendor concentration can happen in fast-growing cities

In rapidly growing cities, municipal spending often clusters among a limited number of vendors because certain spending categories dominate, including infrastructure, utilities, engineering, professional services, and major capital projects.

That makes clear and searchable vendor-payment reporting more important, not because concentration proves wrongdoing, but because it reduces confusion and limits misinformation.

### 4. We found no evidence of corruption or illegality

This point is critical.

Based on what is publicly accessible online, this review found:

• no proof of kickbacks  
• no proof of bribery  
• no proof of conflicts of interest  
• no proof of procurement violations  

Any claim beyond that would be speculation, and this review does not engage in speculation.

### 5. Public payment data exists, but it is not easy to use for service-by-service verification

Princeton publishes financial and payment data online, including check register archives.

However, based on what is currently posted, confirming or disproving HVAC exclusivity over time would require substantial manual work, including downloading multiple files across periods and filtering them by HVAC or mechanical-related vendors and transactions. In some cases, vendor names or line items may not clearly show the service category.

In simple terms:

• the city’s online information provides some visibility  
• but it does not currently provide an easy, resident-friendly way to answer vendor-frequency questions for specific service categories without significant spreadsheet-level analysis  

That gap matters.

## What This Actually Points To

This review does not point to corruption.

It points to something more basic:

**a transparency usability gap**

When residents cannot easily see:

• which vendors are paid  
• how often  
• for what services  
• under what agreements  

reasonable questions arise, even if nothing improper is happening.

In a rapidly growing city like Princeton, where contracting volume tends to increase, process clarity matters as much as outcomes.

Trust is built when residents can independently verify how decisions are made.

## Why This Is a Legitimate Journalistic Inquiry

Investigative journalism is not defined by accusations.

It is defined by verification, restraint, and accountability.

This review:

• took a tip seriously without amplifying unproven claims  
• checked applicable law and published policies  
• reviewed publicly accessible payment reporting  
• distinguished facts from unknowns  
• refused to infer wrongdoing without evidence  
• identified concrete documents that would conclusively answer the question  

## What Happens Next

To conclusively answer the question raised, the next step is straightforward and document-based:

• a vendor payment history specifically scoped to HVAC or mechanical services, ideally in a searchable format  
• copies of any HVAC or mechanical service agreements, contracts, or work authorizations  
• any bid documents, procurement method documentation, or written justifications used where applicable  

These records typically exist and are generally subject to public disclosure under Texas public information law. Once reviewed, they would either:

• confirm competitive, documented procurement  
• or show patterns that deserve further explanation  

## Why This Matters

This is not really about HVAC.

It is about how a city explains its decisions to the people who fund them.

Transparency protects:

• residents  
• city staff  
• elected officials  
• reputable contractors  

When processes are clear, trust grows. When processes are opaque or difficult to verify, suspicion fills the gap.

## Bottom Line

Princeton has formal procurement rules.

Texas law now uses a $100,000 general Chapter 252 threshold.

Public payment data exists.

But for residents trying to answer a basic question about vendor frequency in a specific service category, the city’s current public information is difficult to search and difficult to verify without significant manual work.

That is not proof of corruption.

It is a transparency usability problem.

And in a fast-growing city, usability is part of accountability.
